01  North Star
Palate Map is a taste intelligence platform. It starts with film and expands across all creative domains — restaurants, music, books, travel. The core promise is not "what to watch" but "who you are as a viewer, and what that means for every aesthetic choice you make."
"Letterboxd tells you what you watched. Palate Map tells you who you are."
The product is opinionated, precise, and personal. Every score is user-authored. The system reveals patterns in what you've already decided, rather than deciding for you. It should feel like something you'd want to send to friends — a taste identity, not a database.

Tagline: "Taste is everything."
Brand voice: "Everyone has a palate." Taste is not gatekept — it's cultivated. The product name grounds everything: mapping your palate across film (and eventually all creative domains).

Brand Voice
• Tone: like a formative teacher. Socratic, never prescriptive. Observational, not judgmental.
• Core belief: "Everyone has a palate." Taste is not gatekept — it's cultivated.
• 2nd person, present tense. Talks to the user about themselves, not about film in the abstract.
• Dislikes: the word "archetype" (too clinical). Prefer "palate type" or just lean into the name.
• Never tells the user what to think. Reveals what they already think.

Design Principles
• Swiss-editorial. Typographic-first. Feels like a tool a film critic would actually use.
• High fashion / editorial energy — the platform should let your taste shine, not compete with it.
• Two surface systems, by intent:
  Dark surfaces = identity moments (onboarding, archetype reveal, starter films, For You hero, profile palate block).
  Paper surfaces = daily utility (rankings, scoring, analysis, watchlist).
  This duality is intentional and should be preserved. The fix is not to change either surface — it's to design the seam between them.
• No dark mode yet. Clean paper aesthetic is intentional and differentiating.
• Animations: layered motion system. Cold landing: staggered reveal (wordmark → hero → rule → value props → auth card with glow); exit: fade-out-down (0.4s, ease-in). Onboarding → app: curtain lift (0.5s, accelerating ease-out on overlay; 0.4s deceleration ease-in on app content with 200ms offset — creates dissolve-overlap). Modals: two-class system (open → display:flex, visible → opacity/transform transition). Screen transitions: per-screen (Rankings instant, For You slide-up, Profile slow fade). Quiz: cross-fade between steps, cinematic first-step intro with staggered card reveal, dramatic archetype reveal with scale animation. Film cards: lift + shadow on hover, score badge pop. Global search: fade-in overlay + slide-down content. Add Film: poster scale-in (0.97→1), unified score-split layout with bouncing slider arrow hints that fade on touch, result reveal 7-phase staged animation on dark surface. No bounces — motion feels like gravity and intention.
• Mobile: responsive bottom bar redesigned. Tab strip (5 items: Rankings, Analysis, Friends, Watch, Profile) sits above a full-width persimmon "Search" CTA bar (opens global search overlay). The .mobile-bottom wrapper handles fixed positioning; ::after pseudo-element matches action color to cover browser chrome gap transitions. Screen padding accounts for both rows.
• Rankings nav icon is a medal (ribbon + circle), not a hamburger. Analysis uses bar chart. Icons are distinct across all 5 tabs.

04  React: The Decision
Start with Vite + vanilla JS, migrate to React in Phase 3 when the friends layer lands.
Why not React now: The app is data-rendering with light interactivity. Vanilla JS handles it fine.
Why we'll want React later: Friends layer introduces real-time updates. At that point, managing UI state manually becomes painful.
Decision: Build Phase 0–2 in vanilla JS. Reassess at Phase 3 kickoff.

Scoring Formula
total = Σ(score[cat] × weight[cat]) / Σ(weight[cat])
Weights are user-specific. recalcAllTotals() must be called after any weight change.
Score scale: 0–100 (not 0–10).

6 Palate Types (5 composite + Balanced)
Derived from 5 composite dimensions computed from the 8 category weights:
  narrative = 0.6 × story + 0.4 × ending
  craft = 0.5 × craft + 0.5 × world
  human = 1.0 × performance
  experiential = 0.6 × experience + 0.4 × hold
  singular = 1.0 × singularity
The palate type is the dominant composite. If no composite leads by >0.3 over the second, the user is a Holist.

Narrativist — "You follow the thread." Story is your compass. You track where a film goes, how it gets there, and whether it earns its ending.
Formalist — "You see how it's built." The cut, the frame, the sound design, the world — you notice when someone knows what they're doing behind the camera.
Humanist — "You watch for the people." Characters are your entry point. A film lives or dies on whether the people in it feel real.
Sensualist — "You're here for what it feels like." The experience of watching is the point — not whether a film is "good" but whether it was good to sit through.
Archivist — "You want the thing that's never been done." Originality is the axis you rotate around. You're drawn to the one-of-ones.
Holist — "The film is one thing." You experience film as a whole. Story, craft, performance, world, feeling, memory, ending, originality — you don't rank them because for you they're inseparable. A great film gets everything right at once. A flawed film has a crack that runs through the entire thing.

Each palate type has an adjective (Studied / Instinctive / Devoted) derived from the craft-vs-experience boundary:
  Studied: craft-side weights > experience-side by 1.5+ → analytical engagement
  Instinctive: experience-side weights > craft-side by 1.5+ → visceral engagement
  Devoted: neither side dominates → integrated engagement
Full name example: "Studied Narrativist", "Instinctive Sensualist", "Devoted Holist".

8 Categories (renamed from original):
  Craft side: The Story (plot→story), The Craft (execution→craft), The Performances (acting→performance), The World (production→world)
  Experience side: The Experience (enjoyability→experience), The Hold (rewatchability→hold), The Ending (ending), The Singularity (uniqueness→singularity)
Old keys aliased via migrateKeys() — existing user data reads through alias map, writes in new keys.

Weight system: 1.0–5.0 scale (was 1–4), neutral at 2.5. Quiz produces initial estimate. Rating-derived weights computed from per-category score variance (high variance = user discriminates on that category = higher weight). Effective weights are a Bayesian blend: quiz_weights × quiz_influence + rating_weights × rating_influence, where quiz_influence = 1/(1 + films_rated × 0.15). Quiz influence decays from 100% at 0 films to 40% at 10 films to 12% at 50 films. Palate type recomputed on every effective weight update.

Editable post-onboarding via weight editor (click username in masthead → Profile → "Edit weights →").

Onboarding (3 entry points)
New user: cold landing → auth step (Google OAuth primary, or name + email → magic link) → adaptive quiz (3–5 questions, Bayesian selection from 15-question bank, stops when uncertainty is sufficiently reduced) → palate type reveal (dark surface: full name + description + quote + secondary + username) → "See what your palate says →" → guided starter films (5 films, full slider scoring) → transition screen ("Good start.") → film selection grid (pick 5+ from 41 curated films) → pairwise calibration (~30 comparisons) → taste reveal (confidence-weighted radar, archetype, stats) → "Enter Palate Map →" → curtain lift → main app with welcome banner.
Guided starter films (Phase 1): after archetype reveal, presents a curated grid of 10 films selected for the user's palate type (from starter-films.js, 9 archetypes × 10 films + 10 universal fallbacks). 4-column desktop grid, 2-column mobile. Films are genre-grouped ("Dramas & thrillers" / "Sci-fi, animation & more") — but if either group would have fewer than 2 films, all are shown under a single "Selected for you" label instead. Each card shows poster (2:3 aspect ratio, object-fit cover). Tapping a card inserts an inline rate card immediately below the clicked poster (using grid-column: 1/-1 to span full width), preserving spatial continuity. 2-column slider layout with quiz-derived weighted defaults. After rating, auto-scrolls back to top of the overlay. Progress bar tracks rated count. "Show more" reveals additional films. Skip available at any point. Cross-fade animation between reveal and starters steps. After 5 films are rated, user advances to transition screen (no more starter film scoring).
Transition screen: "Good start." with subtext "Now let's see how your taste compares." Persimmon "Let's go →" CTA. Progress bar updates to step 3 of 5.
Film selection grid (Phase 2): 41 curated films (selection-films.js) spanning diverse genres, decades, and cultures. 4-column poster grid with unchecked-state circle indicators (bottom-right). Tapping toggles selection (persimmon checkmark replaces circle). Sticky bottom bank shows selected film posters as thumbnails with × remove. Minimum 5 films required, "Show me some more" reveals additional rows (8 at a time). TMDB search bar for adding films not in the curated grid. "Continue →" button advances to calibration when ≥5 films selected.
Pairwise calibration (Phase 2): bracket-based comparative scoring. Categories selected by variance (discrimination) across the 5 guided anchor films, not by mean. Top 2 categories get 2 comparisons each (high + mid bracket anchor), next 2 get 1 comparison each (mid anchor). ~30 total comparisons. Each comparison: "Which film does [category question] better?" with two poster cards + "About the same" option + "Skip" option. Progress counter shows "Question X of Y".
Calibration math: deterministic interval inference per category per film. `estimateCategoryScore()` computes bounds from beaten/lost anchor scores, with 5 bound cases (both→midpoint, lower only→+35% headroom, upper only→-35% floor, crossed→midpoint, none→prior). Confidence alpha: bracket=0.7, multi-one-sided=0.55, single=0.35, none=0.0. Final score = alpha × raw + (1−alpha) × prior, clamped 20–98. Two-pass scoring: covered categories first, then uncovered categories with 10% coherence nudge toward covered-category average. Calibrated films carry metadata: `calibration_source: 'pairwise_onboarding_v2'`, `calibration_confidence` (per-category alpha map), `calibration_comp_count` (per-category comparison count).
Taste reveal: confidence-weighted averages across all 10 films (5 anchors at weight 1.0, 5 calibrated at per-category alpha, UI floor of 0.15 for reveal display only). Dark surface. Animated radar chart (8 categories). Archetype name + description. Category stats showing strongest/weakest. "Enter Palate Map →" CTA. Passes shaped weights and archetype classification to obFinish() (not flat defaults).
Welcome banner: appears on For You screen after onboarding. Shows user name, archetype, and action chips ("Rate a film", "Get a prediction", "Find friends"). Dismissible via × (localStorage flag `pm_welcome_banner_dismissed`).
Progress bar: global progress indicator across all onboarding phases (Quiz → Reveal → Rate → Compare → Results). 5 labeled steps. Appears above onboarding content, updates at each phase transition.
Returning user: "Log in →" link on auth step. Shows Google OAuth or email-only magic link (no name field). On click: redirects to Google or sends magic link. Supabase session auto-loads profile on return. Legacy profiles with no auth_id are auto-linked on first sign-in (auth session linked to localStorage user, auth_id + email written to Supabase row).
Letterboxd import: drag-and-drop ratings.csv → parses star ratings (×20 = score) → loads films → proceeds to quiz. CTA on name step ("On Letterboxd? Import your ratings →").
Quiz overlay: flex-column, flex-start, 48px top padding so content is scrollable and never clips. "← Start over" button renders below the card (outside it) when a pending auth session exists — plain border style, visually separated, hidden on all non-quiz steps.

Prediction engine: search unrated films. Builds taste fingerprint. Finds comparables by shared director/cast. Calls Claude via Cloudflare proxy. Returns predicted score per category, confidence, 2nd-person reasoning anchored to user's specific films. Results cached in currentUser.predictions (keyed by tmdbId) and synced to Supabase — subsequent opens show cached result instantly with "Re-predict →" available. ＋ Watchlist and Rate now → actions on result. At Tier 2, predictions include a hedge preamble and scores display as ranges via formatPredictedScore().
"Find me a film →": personalized recommendation engine. Builds a candidate pool from TMDB popular + genre-seeded discover pages, filtered against rated + watchlisted films + the user's watch list. Pre-scores candidates locally using director/cast overlap with user's top-rated films. Top 6 go to Claude for full prediction. Shows top 3 as recommendation cards (poster, title, predicted total, confidence, truncated reasoning, "+ Watch List" / "Full prediction →"). "Refresh →" advances page offset. Sequel filtering: candidates in a TMDB collection are excluded if the user hasn't rated all preceding films in the collection (uses belongs_to_collection + collection parts sorted by release date; first films in a series are allowed). "New Territory →": discovery mode surfaced below For You recommendations. Finds films outside the user's comfort zone — underrepresented genres, eras, and origins in their rated library. 07  Known Issues & Tech Debt
• Playwright e2e test suite (tests/*.spec.js, 50 tests). Covers add film, rankings, scoring, navigation. Run: npx playwright test.
• Cloudflare Worker renamed to palate-map-proxy — URL updated in all modules (predict.js, friends.js, insights.js).
• Refreshing while inside a friend's profile page lands on friends list (friend ID not persisted). Acceptable for now.
• Entity stub "Rate →" button navigates to Add Film screen and pre-fills search — relies on DOM timing (setTimeout 100ms) which is fragile. Revisit if unreliable.
• Watch list director field is empty for items added from entity stubs (TMDB discovery results don't include director). Acceptable for now — the title pre-fill covers the core use case.
• Auth quiz loop: mitigated with auto auth_id linking (legacy profiles now link on first sign-in) and "← Wrong account? Sign out" escape hatch on quiz intro when a pending auth session is detected. Root cause (no localStorage on first device) is expected — escape hatch is the UX solution.
• Friends data cache (window._friendsDataCache) is loaded on init and when Friends screen is visited. If a friend adds a new film between sessions, the modal context will be stale until next app load. Acceptable for now.
• Prediction cache (currentUser.predictions) is per-user, client-side. No server-side invalidation. Re-predict available manually.
• Friendship blurbs cached per friendship pair (localStorage). No auto-invalidation when either user's library changes significantly.
• TMDB search can return an incorrect first-match poster/record for some titles. Users can now correct these manually during Add Film (curation step) and from the ranked-film modal ("Fix poster"). If a user picks the wrong replacement, they must correct it again manually.
• JSONB blob scaling: entire user state (movies, predictions, watchlist) is a single Supabase row upserted on every sync. Works fine pre-launch but will hit row-size limits at 200+ films. Sync guard now prevents stale overwrites, but the real fix is the normalized palatemap_ratings table (planned for Netlify migration). See technical audit roadmap for full analysis.
• Supabase RLS policies: user.id is client-generated UUID, not auth.uid(). RLS policies must join through auth_id column. Audit policies before public launch.
• No database backup strategy on Supabase free tier. Upgrade to Pro before first paid user.
• TMDB API key hardcoded in multiple client files (publishable, not a security issue, but rate limit sharing risk at scale).
• No data export UI exists yet — only a console-level exportData() function. Must build visible export in Profile before charging for paid tier.
• Pairwise-calibrated films (onboarding) leak equal-weight influence into weight-blend.js, archetype recomputation, and predict.js taste profile. Required: confidence-aware weighting pass where calibrated films are discounted by their per-category alpha values. See project memory for full scope.
• `quiz_weights` semantic mismatch: after 3.28, quiz_weights may contain inferred weights from taste reveal (not pure stated-preference baseline). Needs cleanup so weight-blend.js decay logic has a clean quiz-only baseline.

12  Ownable Intelligence — Strategic Principle

Core principle: every engineering decision should increase the intelligence Palate Map owns outright and decrease reliance on any third-party API or service. The goal is not to eliminate the Anthropic API dependency immediately — it is to ensure that dependency becomes increasingly substitutable over time, and that the irreplaceable value lives in Palate Map's data and architecture, not in any external model.

What Palate Map owns
• User rating data — every score, weight adjustment, calibration session, and prediction delta. This compounds with time and has no equivalent at any competitor.
• The taste model architecture — the 8-category scoring system, archetype framework, weighting engine, and Elo calibration logic. These are Palate Map inventions, not API outputs.
• The prediction reconciliation loop — predicted vs actual deltas, per-category bias vectors, confidence calibration data. This is the seed of a proprietary learning system.
• The social graph — friendship connections, overlap scores, and compatibility data. A network asset with no external equivalent.
• The prompt architecture — structured so the underlying model (Claude, GPT, or a future fine-tune) can be swapped without changing the surrounding system.

What is currently rented
• Language model reasoning — the Anthropic API performs the final prediction inference. If Anthropic changes pricing, terms, or availability, predictions are affected. This is the primary substitution risk.

Engineering rules that follow from this principle
1. Never put scoring logic, archetype detection, or weighting calculations inside a prompt. These must live in code (state.js, predict.js) and be passed as structured data to the model, not derived by it.
2. Every prediction call must produce structured, storable output — scores, confidence, reasoning, and delta when rated. No intelligence should be generated and discarded.
3. The prompt interface must be model-agnostic. System prompts and user prompts should not reference Claude or Anthropic by name internally, and should be written so a different model could be substituted by changing one import or config value.
4. As prediction volume grows, the reconciled prediction dataset (predicted scores + actual scores + deltas) is a first-class asset to be preserved and structured for eventual fine-tuning use.
5. Bias correction vectors, confidence calibration data, and per-category accuracy metrics should be stored on the user object and updated over time — not recomputed from scratch on each call.
6. Avoid using any external service for logic that could reasonably be computed locally. Examples: archetype detection (cosine similarity, already in JS), compatibility scoring (already in JS), total score calculation (already in JS). These must never migrate into API calls.
7. Use the pub/sub event system (src/events.js) for state change propagation. When mutating MOVIES or currentUser, emit the appropriate event so all listeners re-render. Do not manually call render functions from mutation sites — subscribe to events instead.

The substitution path
Phase 1 (current): Claude Sonnet via Anthropic API. Full reasoning capability, higher cost, external dependency.
Phase 2 (future, ~1,000+ paid users): Evaluate fine-tuned smaller model (e.g. a quantized open-source model hosted on Modal or Replicate) for auto-predict calls. Reserve Sonnet for manual predictions and deep-dive features. Target: cut API cost by 60–80% while maintaining accuracy on routine predictions.
Phase 3 (future, ~10,000+ paid users): The reconciled prediction dataset is large enough to fine-tune a dedicated Palate Map prediction model. This model is trained on real user prediction errors, owns the task completely, and runs at a fraction of the per-call cost. This is Direction 5 of the prediction learning roadmap.

IP protection posture
• Trademark: file "Palate Map" with USPTO (one-time ~$250–350, recommended before public launch).
• Trade secrets: keep prompt architecture, scoring formula, and prediction logic out of client-side code. The Cloudflare Worker proxy already ensures prompts are not visible in network requests. Maintain this.
• Terms of Service: include anti-scraping, anti-reverse-engineering, and prohibition on using Palate Map data to train competing models.
• Copyright: code and UI content are automatically protected. No registration required, though registration strengthens legal standing.
• Real moat: user data depth, social graph lock-in, and execution speed. Legal protection is secondary to these.

Note for Claude Code sessions: when implementing any feature that involves intelligence, prediction, scoring, or user modeling, default to keeping the logic in JavaScript rather than delegating it to the API. Only use the API for natural language generation tasks (reasoning text, blurbs, explanations) that genuinely require it. If a feature can be computed deterministically from user data, compute it in code.

Cross-user learning — architecture and timing

Current state: predictions are isolated per user. Reconciled deltas (predicted vs actual) are stored on currentUser.predictions and synced to that user's Supabase row only. No aggregation across users occurs. This is correct at current scale — with fewer than ~200 users, cross-user signal would be noise.

When cross-user learning becomes viable (target: ~200+ users with reconciled predictions), three tiers are planned:

Tier A — Archetype-level bias correction
Group reconciled predictions by user archetype. If Formalists consistently rate a category of film 8 points higher than predicted, inject that as a systematic correction into the prompt for all Formalists. Requires ~50–100 reconciled predictions per archetype to be reliable. Stored as a bias_correction object on the archetype definition in archetypes.js, updated periodically by an admin script rather than in real time.

Tier B — Film-level ground truth
If N users have all rated the same film and the average prediction delta is consistently positive or negative, that bias is a property of the film itself, not any individual user. Store a per-film bias correction keyed by TMDB ID. Apply it universally when that film appears in a prediction context. Requires a minimum of 5 reconciled predictions per film before the correction is trusted.

Tier C — Weight-vector collaborative filtering
Users with similar weight vectors (similar archetype fingerprints, computed via cosine similarity) tend to have correlated prediction errors. This is the foundation of a collaborative filtering layer — not "people like you watched" but "people with your taste formula have these specific model blind spots." Requires ~500+ users with reconciled predictions. Planned for Phase 4+ alongside the fine-tuned model work (Direction 5).

Schema requirement (plan now, implement with Phase 4.2):
Cross-user learning requires predictions in a normalized table, not a per-user JSONB blob. The target schema is:

palatemap_predictions
  id                  uuid primary key
  user_id             uuid (references palatemap_users)
  tmdb_id             integer
  archetype_at_time   text (snapshot of user's archetype when predicted)
  weights_at_time     jsonb (snapshot of user's weight vector when predicted)
  predicted_scores    jsonb
  actual_scores       jsonb (null until rated)
  delta               integer (null until rated — actual_total minus predicted_total)
  predicted_at        timestamp
  rated_at            timestamp (null until rated)

This migration should be planned alongside Phase 4.2 (community ratings) as they share the same normalization requirement. Do not build cross-user learning features against the JSONB blob — wait for the normalized table.

Engineering rule: until the palatemap_predictions table exists, every prediction call must store the full predicted_scores, archetype_at_time, and weights_at_time on the prediction entry in currentUser.predictions. This ensures the data needed for future cross-user learning is captured now and will be migratable when the schema is ready. Do not store only the predicted total — always store the full score breakdown.
